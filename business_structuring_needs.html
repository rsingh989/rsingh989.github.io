YOUR BUSINESS STRUCTURING NEEDS A LOT OF FOCUS TOO

Some feel that the fundamental part of every lean startup is working on the business model, which makes it a fundamental part of every founderâ€™s mindset. However, if you are wise enough, you would know that just having a good business plan is not enough to get your startup off the ground and make it successful.

Even though the business model canvas is an integral part of the visual process that guide you to communicate your idea and organise your thoughts, some critics have argued that it is often a waste of time and something that is largely demanded by investors, who in turn often choose to ignore what you produce instead.

1. Build a solid organisational structure

As the INSEAD paper suggests, the more a founder focuses on the business model, the more emphasis is placed on the idea instead of where it should be, like the business, especially its structure. We are witnessing a spectacular growth in the startup world, with new ventures popping up everywhere, especially since the pandemic started. There is an emphasis on speed, with minimum viable products that are specifically designed to test the value proposition and pivot rapidly to integrate your learning about the future iterations.

It is this hurried behaviour that can often lead the entrepreneurs to overlook the basic organisational questions, such as how to delegate the work among their team members, and how to keep them motivated. There is a tendency to ignore their tasks which are as vital as formulating a great business plan and hope that everything will fall into place at the end.

2. The managers get involved

More often than not, managers are delegated by the founders to try to get their organisation into shape. An analysis of eight companies by researchers over an 18-month period revealed that all of the companies had implemented some basic form of organisation design which covered task division and allocation, reward provision, information exchange and grievance resolution. While some of these had been consciously planned, the others were implemented based on what felt instinctive or natural.

Only two of the founders were actually able to articulate any real logic behind their organisational structure, even though both of them described a largely ad hoc and emergent structure.

3. Looking at the big picture

During the course of those 18 months, researchers were also able to determine any changes that were made to the business, whether it was in the form of business model or their organisational structure. In all, they found out that there were 121 situations which were related to either of these factors that resulted in a change in the business in terms of its organisation.

Even though most of these changes were incremental at best, they would often coalesce around the route that was the fastest and cheapest. This is a common ground for entrepreneurs who try to solve problems with as little effort as possible, even if this fails to tackle the underlying problem.

4. Lack of focus

The underlying problem, which is the lack of real focus given to the organisational structure by the founders, who mostly try to rely on their instincts and wing it. This is not a great strategy when it comes to creating a business that is not only successful, but also scalable.

According to the researchers, the first step is to take the matter of creating an organisational design for your business seriously. If you cannot find a way to create your own framework, you can always rely on the organisational design canvas, which operates in a way similar to the business model canvas, allowing entrepreneurs to think systemically about the key issues which affect the business.

It is well-known that firms will struggle to grow without a great business model in place, but we are also reminded of the more practical realities of the world, that organisation structure is equally as important as the business model. It cannot be stressed enough that for your business to succeed phenomenally, you need to focus on both as a founder.
